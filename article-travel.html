Travel

A Train, a Thermos, and the Long Way Home

In praise of the journey that refuses to hurry — and the strangers who make it worth the wait.

DA
Dev Anand · Grade 11
June 2026 · 4 min read
illustration

There is a particular sound a train makes in the first ten minutes after it leaves a city — a slow, sceptical clatter, as though it has not yet decided whether the trip is worth it. I have come to love that sound. I have come to love, in fact, the entire inefficient enterprise: the eighteen hours it takes to cover a distance a plane swallows in ninety minutes, the unaccountable halts at stations whose names I cannot pronounce, the way the whole machine seems to be in no particular hurry to arrive anywhere at all. My father calls this a waste of a day. I have begun to suspect it is the opposite.

We are, as a family, in flight from the slow train. There is a flight from everything now. We fly to weddings, to funerals, to the grandmother who would rather we took our time. The aeroplane has taught us to think of the in-between as something to be deleted — a grey corridor of recycled air and a man asking, twice, whether we would like the chicken or the paneer. You step into a metal tube in one place and step out in another, and the country between the two places simply does not happen to you. It is edited out. You arrive unmarked by the distance you have crossed.

The train refuses to do this. The train insists that the distance is real, that it must be felt in the small of your back and the crick of your neck, that the land between Nagpur and home is not an abstraction but a long, dusty, sun-stunned fact you are obliged to sit through. And sitting through it, I have learned, is the entire point.

The plane abolishes the country between two places. The train makes you pay for it, mile by mile, in the only honest currency — time.

The Economy of the Thermos

My mother packs a thermos of chai for every journey, and the thermos has a sort of authority on the train that it never has at home. At home it is a flask. On the train it becomes an institution. Around the third hour, when the initial novelty has worn thin and the snacks have been eaten in a fit of premature enthusiasm, the thermos is produced. It is unscrewed with ceremony. The first cup is poured for whichever stranger is sitting opposite, because that is simply what one does, and the stranger protests, and is overruled, and accepts.

And then a curious thing happens, as it always happens. The stranger, having been given chai, is now a person. He is a retired schoolteacher from Wardha, or a young man going home to a marriage he is not entirely sure about, or a grandmother carrying a steel tin of laddoos she will insist you try. The chai has dissolved the membrane that the city installs between people. You would never speak to this man at a traffic light. Here, somewhere past Itarsi, you know the names of his children.

This is the thing the aeroplane cannot give you, and would not give you if it could. On a flight you are a passenger, sealed, headphoned, willing the person beside you into non-existence. On the train you are a temporary citizen of a small republic that assembles at boarding and dissolves at the destination, and for one long afternoon you owe each other tea and conversation and the occasional shared anxiety about whether the next station is, in fact, the next station.

What the Window Knows

And there is the window. Always the window. The plane gives you cloud and the abstract geometry of fields from too high to mean anything. The train gives you the country at eye level and at walking pace: a boy waving at the train for no reason except that it is a train and he is a boy; a woman carrying water across a field gone gold in the late light; the sudden green shock of a paddy after a long stretch of scrub; a town that exists, apparently, entirely so that the train may slow down and look at it.

You cannot doze through all of it, though you try. The land keeps tapping you on the shoulder. It seems important that you notice it is there — that between your house and your grandmother's house lies all this, these millions of lives and fields and level crossings, none of which you would have crossed paths with had you simply paid for the faster thing.

We have decided, as a civilisation, that the fast thing is the better thing, and most days I believe it too. But I think we lose something we cannot name when we abolish the long way home. We lose the chai, and the schoolteacher from Wardha, and the boy waving at nothing. We arrive on time and unimproved. Give me, instead, the train that cannot be bothered to hurry — and a thermos, and a window, and the patience to let the distance happen to me.
