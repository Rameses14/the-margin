The Tyranny of the Tiffin

A short, affectionate history of the school lunchbox, and the small economy that runs on it.

MJ
Meher Joshi · Grade 9
June 2026 · 3 min read
illustration

Nobody tells you, when you start school, that the most complicated relationship of your day will be with a steel box. The tiffin arrives every morning with the weight of an obligation. It has been packed in the dark, before the rest of the house was properly awake, by a mother who believes — sincerely, against all evidence — that her child will be ravenous by eleven o’clock and possibly faint without four parathas, a katori of sabzi, a banana, and an emergency packet of biscuits “just in case.” I have never once finished a tiffin. I am not sure anyone has. The over-packing is not really about hunger. It is about love, expressed in the only unit my mother fully trusts: surplus.

By the time the lunch bell goes, the classroom has transformed into something between a marketplace and a small, tense diplomatic summit. Boxes come out. Lids are removed with the casual confidence of people who already know exactly what is inside and want everyone to see it anyway. And the trading begins.

The Unwritten Exchange Rate

There is an economy here, fully formed and ruthlessly precise, that no adult has ever taught us and no adult could improve. Everyone knows, instinctively, that a single homemade aloo paratha is worth roughly two store-bought anything. A boiled egg trades poorly. A slice of cake trades like gold. Nobody wants the apple, ever, and the apple therefore functions as a kind of currency you can only spend on yourself, in private, out of guilt.

The trades are rarely about taste. They are about novelty — about wanting, fiercely and irrationally, whatever is in the other person’s box precisely because it is not in yours. My mother’s thepla, which I would not touch at home, becomes a coveted asset the moment my deskmate eyes it. Scarcity does to a lunchbox what it does to everything else. The thing you have is dull. The thing across the aisle is fascinating.

No one ever wants what is in their own tiffin. This is not about food. It is the first economics any of us ever learn.

And there are rules, fierce and unspoken. You do not open your box and eat alone; that is a kind of crime. You offer the first bite to whoever is nearest, who refuses, and you insist, and they accept — the exact same ceremony, I have since noticed, that the grown-ups perform with tea. Sharing is mandatory; hoarding is remembered. A boy who once ate an entire packet of chips without offering a single one is, to this day, spoken of in the past tense, as though something in him had died.

The Kid Whose Tiffin Everyone Wants

Every class has one. Ours is a quiet girl named Tara, whose mother sends, on rotation, a parade of small perfect things: lemon rice still warm in its foil, dosa folded into neat triangles, a dabba of payasam on somebody’s birthday that she distributes with the grave generosity of a head of state. Tara herself eats almost nothing. She does not need to. Her power lies entirely in distribution. To be near Tara at lunch is to be near the centre of the economy, and the seats around her are negotiated, I promise you, days in advance.

I used to resent my own tiffin — its sameness, its sheer relentless volume, the way it announced, every single day, that someone at home had been thinking about whether I would be hungry. I resent it less now. Soon enough, I’m told, the steel box gives way to a canteen card and a queue and food that nobody packed for you in the dark. No surplus. No emergency biscuits. Nothing to trade.

So I have started, against my own nature, to eat the parathas. All of them, sometimes. Not because I am hungry — I am almost never hungry — but because one day the tiffin will stop arriving, and I would like to have noticed, while it lasted, exactly how much was inside.
