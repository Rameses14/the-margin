In Defence of the Pigeon, the City’s Most Underrated Mind

We call it a flying rat and shoo it off the balcony — but the bird on the railing has quietly outsmarted us in ways we have spent decades failing to replicate.

SI
Sara Iqbal · Grade 10
June 2026 · 5 min read
illustration

There is a pigeon on the railing outside my window as I write this, and it is, I am sorry to report, doing absolutely nothing impressive. It is bobbing. It is regarding a leaf. Every so often it makes the small unconvincing noise that is the only thing pigeons are famous for. And yet I have come to believe that this dull grey creature, the one we wave off our balconies and blame for everything, is carrying around one of the most quietly remarkable minds in the city — and that our contempt for it is mostly a story about us.

Start with the obvious miracle, the one we have known for three thousand years and somehow stopped finding miraculous. You can take a pigeon, put it in a covered basket, drive it eight hundred kilometres in a direction it has never travelled, release it into a sky it has never seen, and it will come home. Not roughly home. Home — the same ledge, the same cracked drainpipe. We strapped messages to these birds in wars because they were more reliable than the technology we had. The bird on my railing is descended from a courier we trusted with our lives.

A Compass We Still Argue About

How they do it is the part that should embarrass us. Pigeons appear to read the Earth’s magnetic field, sensing the planet itself as a kind of map. They also use the sun, adjusting for the time of day — which means somewhere behind those flat orange eyes is a working clock and the trigonometry to use it. They memorise landmarks, smells, the low rumble of distant infrasound we cannot even hear. Scientists have spent careers and considerable funding trying to settle exactly which of these the pigeon is using, and the honest answer is that the pigeon knows and we still mostly don’t.

We engineered satellites and a network of atomic clocks in orbit to do navigation the pigeon has been doing, for free, off a diet of discarded biscuit.

And then there are the laboratory experiments, which read less like science and more like a series of small public humiliations for our species. Pigeons have been trained to tell a Monet from a Picasso — and, shown new paintings they had never seen, to sort them correctly by artist. They have been taught to distinguish benign from malignant tissue on medical scans, performing, as a flock, about as well as trained human readers. They can recognise individual human faces and, crucially, hold a grudge: shoo a pigeon and it will remember you, specifically, and avoid you later. The flying rat is keeping a list.

What the Contempt Is Really About

So why do we despise it? I think it is because the pigeon broke the unspoken deal. We forgive intelligence in animals that keep their distance — the crow on the wire, the dolphin in the documentary, safely far away and therefore noble. The pigeon did something unforgivable: it moved in. It looked at our ledges and station roofs and decided they were as good as any cliff, ate what we dropped, and thrived. We resent it the way we resent anyone who succeeds by ignoring the rules we pretend to live by. Its great crime is not stupidity. It is that it adapted to us better than we adapted to it.

Look again at the bird on the railing. It has navigated a city built to confuse it, learned which humans throw food and which throw slippers, and worked out — without a syllabus, without a single tuition class — how to live everywhere we live and a few places we can’t. We call it a pest because it is common, and we mistake common for simple, which is the same error that makes us underrate the people who do the city’s quiet work and overrate the ones who merely look the part.

I am not asking you to feed the pigeons; the building society has rules, and so, apparently, does my mother. I am only asking you, the next time one tilts its head at you on a railing, to consider that it might be the one doing the studying. It remembers your face. It found its way here from somewhere you have never been. And it is, by almost every measure that matters, paying closer attention to the world than you are.
