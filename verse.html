Beginnings, in Three Poems

On leaving, arriving, and the rooms in between.

VC
The Senior Verse Circle
June 2026
What the Doorway Keeps

— by Aisha Khan, Grade 11

The night before we left, my mother taped the corners of the boxes twice, as if the tape could hold the kitchen still inside. I walked the hallway counting every scuff — the place the bike fell, the place I learned the word for stubborn, the place the light came in at four. A house remembers nothing. It is we who carry doorways folded in our coats. I pressed one palm flat to the cooling wall and felt it not say goodbye. That was the gift: it let me leave the way a tide leaves sand, taking only what it needed of me and leaving the rest to dry into a shape I would not recognise but would still call home.
First Bell

— by Tanvi Deshmukh, Grade 9

I practised my name in the mirror so it would not shake when I said it. New shoes, too white, announcing every step I had not yet earned. The corridor was a river of people who already knew each other — their laughter a current I stood beside, holding my schedule like a paddle I did not know how to use. Then a girl with ink on her thumb asked if this was room nine, and I said I think so, and we were both wrong, and being wrong together was the first thing I belonged to.
Inventory of an Empty Room

— by Ishaan Gupta, Grade 12

For a week it held only echo — the radiator’s small complaint, a square of sun that crossed the floor each afternoon like a slow visitor. I gave it a chair. It made the chair look lonelier, so I gave it a lamp, then a book left open to no page, then the smell of toast, then arguments, then the particular quiet after them. A room is not a thing you fill. It is a thing that learns your weight, that keeps the dent your shoulders leave against the wall when you lean back and finally, carefully, begin.
