Issue No. 1 — “Beginnings”

Theme: first attempts, first principles, and the unglamorous work that gets anything started.
Published · 3 of 3 briefed
The throughline. Both pieces this issue look past the headline and ask who actually does the work. The issue pairs a science essay about the labour behind grand ambitions with a film essay about a movie that earns its ending — a mix of one strong argument per writer, not balanced both-sides reporting, because this is an opinion magazine.

Science

Everyone Wants to Go to Mars. Nobody Wants to Do the Dishes.

Abhimanyu Shah · Editor-in-Chief · Published
Edit brief
The plan — subject · angle · what’s discussed A science essay arguing that the real hero of getting to Mars isn’t the flag-planter but the plumber, the gardener, and the recycler. Abhimanyu’s argument: a colony lives or dies on the unglamorous, repetitive work of keeping water clean, air breathable, and waste turned back into something useful — so the future is, first and last, a plumbing problem. Discusses closed-loop life support, who actually keeps a habitat alive, and why we romanticise the launch and ignore the maintenance.
Film

Disclosure Day Is a Spielberg Film for the Believers

Abhimanyu Shah · Editor-in-Chief · Published
Edit brief
The plan — subject · angle · what’s discussed A film essay on Disclosure Day, a Spielberg-style alien-contact picture aimed squarely at the people who want to believe. Abhimanyu’s argument: the film sags badly through its middle act, but its final act is good enough to forgive the drift entirely. Discusses the grammar of the Spielberg wonder-shot, why the second act loses its nerve, and how one extraordinary ending can redeem a flawed film.
Business

Luxury Isn’t Selling Status Anymore. It’s Selling Control.

Aditi Agarwal · Published
Edit brief
The plan — subject · angle · what’s discussed A business essay arguing that luxury is booming only because of a “K-shaped” economy where the rich keep spending while everyone else is squeezed out. Roughly 80% of the sector’s growth comes from price rises, not new customers, and luxury is increasingly sold as security and control — gold and watches pitched as savings — rather than as status, while the aspirational middle is priced out. Aditi’s argument: brands winning on price hikes and scarcity alone are borrowing against their customers’ trust, and the houses that last will be the ones that rebuild real craftsmanship.
