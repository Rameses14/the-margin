The Canteen Economy: What a Samosa Really Costs

A field guide to the only market we trust completely — because we have to eat in it every day at 10:40 sharp.

KM
Kabir Menon · Grade 12
June 2026 · 4 min read
illustration

Economists will tell you that the price of a thing is the number printed beside it, and economists, bless them, have clearly never queued for a samosa. The board behind the counter says ten rupees. This is a polite fiction, like the school motto, and roughly as binding. The real price of a samosa is ten rupees plus the eleven minutes you spend not breathing while a boy from Grade 9 negotiates change with the slow conviction of a hostage negotiator. Money is the smallest thing you pay.

Consider what economists call opportunity cost, which they explain with diagrams and which our canteen explains with despair. There are two windows and four hundred children and exactly twenty-three minutes of recess. To stand in the samosa line is to renounce the field, the gossip, the precise corner of the corridor where a certain person leans. The board says ten rupees. The market says: choose.

And the market is ruthless about it. Scarcity here is not a chapter; it is a lifestyle. The kitchen produces, on a good day, perhaps a hundred and forty samosas, which is enough to feed a hundred and forty children and to disappoint roughly two hundred and sixty. Supply meets demand the way a scooter meets a speed bump — abruptly, and with consequences for everyone behind it.

The Queue Is the Price

This is the part the textbooks miss. We are taught that prices ration scarce goods, that when something runs short the number on the board climbs until the line thins out. But our canteen aunty does not believe in surge pricing. She believes in the line. The samosa stays ten rupees forever, immovable as a saint, and so the rationing happens in the only currency she will accept after cash: your time, your patience, your willingness to stand there while the universe contracts to the width of a steel counter.

Watch the line on a Wednesday and you are watching a price discover itself. The boy who arrives at 10:38 pays two minutes. The boy who ambles up at 10:51, having stopped to be cool near the water cooler, pays in the hard coin of regret — there are no samosas, only the warm tray where samosas recently were, and the aunty’s face, which has seen everything and forgives nothing.

The board says ten rupees. The board is lying. The samosa costs ten rupees, eleven minutes, and the small civic humiliation of asking the person ahead of you to please move up.

There is, too, a second economy layered over the first, and this is where it gets interesting. Among us, the samosa is not merely food. It is money. A favour is worth half a samosa. A particularly damning piece of homework — copied, in full, in your own neat hand — runs you a clean two. When the Grade 11 boys lost their cricket bet last term, the debt was settled in samosas over three weeks, with interest, and nobody thought this strange. The rupee is what we use to buy samosas. The samosa is what we use to buy each other.

The Day the Supplier Was Late

You learn what a currency is worth on the day it disappears. In March the potato supplier’s van broke down somewhere near the highway, and by second period the news had moved through the school faster than any exam result. No samosas. The effect was not hunger — we are children of plenty, there were sandwiches — but something closer to a small monetary crisis. Debts denominated in samosas suddenly had no underlying asset. A boy who was owed three of them spent the afternoon looking, frankly, betrayed, as though his bank had quietly failed.

This is the contrarian thing I want to say, and I will say it standing well clear of the canteen aunty: our school economy is more honest than the real one. Nobody here pretends the price is just the number. We can all see the line. We can all feel the twenty-three minutes draining. The cost is total and visible and paid in full, in public, by everyone, every single day — which is more than you can say for most markets, where the true price hides in the footnotes and arrives, like the supplier’s van, much later than promised.

So the next time someone tells you a samosa costs ten rupees, smile, and pay them the eleven minutes too. It is the most economics you will ever learn standing up.
