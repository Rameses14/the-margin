The Bench Knows Things the Scoreboard Doesn’t

In praise of the boy who never plays, the girl who carries the water, and everyone else the highlight reel forgets.

AN
Arjun Nair · Grade 11
June 2026 · 4 min read
illustration

There is a particular boy on every school team, and you already know him. He arrives before the cones are out and leaves after the nets are rolled. He has the cleanest kit in the squad, because it is the least used. He knows the coach’s temper, the goalkeeper’s superstitions, and exactly how many minutes are left before the bell — and he has played, this entire season, for a grand total of eleven. I have decided he is the most interesting person on the field.

We are taught, more or less from birth, to watch the ball. Where the ball goes, the eyes go; where the eyes go, the cheering goes; and where the cheering goes, the memory follows. This is why nobody at our school can name a single substitute from the Inter-House final but everyone can describe, in operatic detail, the last-minute header that won it. The scoreboard is a machine designed to forget most of what happens in front of it. It records goals. It does not record the seventeen practices in forty-one-degree heat that made the goal possible, and it certainly does not record the boy who stood at the edge of all seventeen, clapping.

I am not, let me be clear, mounting a defence of mediocrity. Effort that goes nowhere is not automatically noble; sometimes a kid sits on the bench because he genuinely cannot kick straight, and pretending otherwise insults everyone. But there is a difference between losing and not winning, and an even larger difference between not winning and not mattering — and it is that last gap, the one our trophy cabinets are silent about, that I think school sport is secretly, accidentally, beautifully for.

Consider what the substitute actually does. He shows up to a thing that owes him nothing. No one is keeping his stats. No college is calling. The crowd, such as it is — three parents and a dog — will not chant his name. And he comes anyway, every Tuesday and Thursday, and he runs the same drills as the starters, and when the captain’s voice goes hoarse it is somehow his that picks up the count. If you want to find out whether a person can be loyal to something larger than his own applause, do not watch the striker. Watch the boy warming up at the seventy-ninth minute for a game that is already, mathematically, lost.

Winning teaches you that you were right. The bench teaches you how to stay when you were not even consulted.

What the water bottle saw

My own qualification for writing this is unimpressive and total: I have warmed that bench. In Grade 9 I made the football squad in the way one makes a queue — by standing in it long enough. I attended every session. I played, across two terms, about as much football as you could fit into a long advertisement. And I want to report something that surprised me, which is that I was not, for most of it, miserable.

Because the bench, it turns out, is the best seat in the house for the only thing worth watching, which is people. From there you learn that the cocky winger is terrified before every match and hides it by talking. You learn that the coach who screams is gentlest with the worst player. You learn the exact texture of a team’s mood — when it is brittle, when it is loose, when one quiet word will hold it together and one loud one will crack it — and none of this appears in any result. The starters are too busy being the result to notice it. The bench has nothing to do but pay attention, and attention, I have come to believe, is its own quiet form of love.

A modest reckoning

So here is what I would put on the scoreboard if it had more room, which it never will. I would record the girl who carries the medical kit to every away match and has memorised which boy faints at the sight of his own knee. I would record the reserve keeper who spent four years learning a position he would play for ninety total minutes, and played them flawlessly. I would record the substitutes who, on the bus home from a thrashing, are the first to start singing — not because they are delusional, but because somebody has to decide the day was not wasted, and it is usually them.

Belonging is not handed out at the medal ceremony. It is built, unglamorously, in the cold half-hour before practice, by the people who keep arriving when no reward arrives with them. The starters will get the photographs. That is fine; they earned them. But years from now, when I have forgotten every score, I suspect I will still remember the boy with the cleanest kit, clapping at the edge of a game he was not in — and I will understand, far too late, that he was the one who actually understood what a team is.

The scoreboard, bless it, will never know his name. We should.
